## Abhaaldrac
##### *He Who Wonders Until Nothing Remains*

{{imageMaskEdge5,--offset:5%,--rotation:90
![Abhaaldrac](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/la-rockoteque/Vestigium/refs/heads/main/assets/art/Dieties/Abhaaldrac.png){position:absolute,top:0,left:-20%,height:100%}
}}

|   |  |
|:-:|:-|
| **Alignment** | Evil |
| **Pantheon** | Vorthirith |
| **Plane** | The Far Cosmos |

Abhaaldrac is the cosmos turned inquisitive, the endless firmament gazing inward upon itsAestari with a curiosity devoid of compassion. He does not hunger, nor hate, nor covet. He wants to know what happens.

Drifting through the Far Cosmos, Abhaaldrac dismantles stars and civilizations alike as one might dismantle a mechanism, not to possess its parts, but to observe the moment it fails. Worlds fall silent beneath his regard, their extinction not an act of cruelty, but of experiment. In Abhaaldrac, the universe tests its own limits and records the result in ash.

From him came Nighragg, Malkuth, and Alcméon, not as deliberate creations, but as byproducts of his cosmic interference. Each bears traces of his paradox: light bound to darkness, structure haunted by absence, motion sustained by loss. Abhaaldrac does not guide them. He barely remembers them.

Among the Vorthirith, Abhaaldrac was once sovereign, but sovereignty bored him. He abandoned command in favor of observation, leaving behind a lineage of entities shaped by his unanswered questions. To invoke Abhaaldrac is not to invite destruction, but to become part of an experiment whose outcome will not be mourned.

## Agramon

{{imageMaskEdge3,--offset:5%,--rotation:270
![Agramon](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/la-rockoteque/Vestigium/refs/heads/main/assets/art/Dieties/Agramon.png){position:absolute,top:0,right:-20%,height:100%}
}}

|   |  |
|:-:|:-|
| **Alignment** | Evil |
| **Symbol** | The Gladiator’s Helm |
| **Pantheon** | Children of the Moeders |
| **Plane** | The Murmuring Depths |

A god of **violence, wrath, and the inescapable bonds of blood**, Agramon is a figure of contradiction—both destroyer and protector, tyrant and guardian. He is war **not for glory, but for dominance**, for the raw assertion of power through conflict. He is worshipped by conquerors, warlords, and those who see battle as the purest form of control.

Depicted as a **towering warrior clad in blood-streaked bronze**, his presence looms over history like a storm that never ceases. He does not fight for justice or honor, but for the **ineluctable truth that strength rules all**. Yet, beneath his relentless brutality lies something deeper—a **twisted grasp on love, kinship, and the unbreakable chains of family.** He binds as fiercely as he destroys, enforcing loyalty with the same force that he crushes defiance.

## Alcmeon
##### *Veilen Vagrant, The Captain of the Veil, The Vagabond*

{{imageMaskEdge5,--offset:5%,--rotation:90
![Alcmeon](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/la-rockoteque/Vestigium/refs/heads/main/assets/art/Dieties/Alcmeon.png){position:absolute,top:0,left:-20%,height:100%}
}}

|   |  |
|:-:|:-|
| **Alignment** | Good |
| **Symbol** | Boat |
| **Plane** | The Veil |

Alcméon, the Vagabond of the Veil, is a liminal figure who drifts along the hidden folds of reality where worlds thin and meanings blur. He sails not upon seas of stars, but across the Veil itsAestari, aboard a vessel that glides between what is and what might be. His boat is fashioned from condensed light and half-remembered matter, its sails woven from refracted possibilities that catch the unseen currents between realms.

As a guardian of travelers, Alcméon watches over those who dare to cross thresholds, slip between planes, or walk paths not meant to be stable. Within the Veil, dangers are subtle and disorienting rather than violent, and it is here that his guidance proves most vital. Those who encounter him speak of near-disasters gently diverted, of doors opening where none should exist, and of journeys altered just enough to avoid becoming lost forever.

His legend is one of quiet wonder rather than spectacle. Alcméon does not dominate the Veil; he listens to it, moving with its shifts and eddies like a practiced mariner reading an unreadable sky. Wise yet playful, patient yet untethered, he reminds wanderers that passage itsAestari is sacred, and that crossing between states of being carries meaning beyond arrival.

To follow Alcméon, even briefly, is to learn that the Veil is not merely a boundary, but a living passage, and that those who respect its uncertainties may find paths unavailable to the anchored and the certain.

## Artume
##### *The Cunning Fox*

{{imageMaskEdge5,--offset:5%,--rotation:90
![Artume](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/la-rockoteque/Vestigium/refs/heads/main/assets/art/Dieties/Artume.png){position:absolute,top:0,left:-20%,height:100%}
}}

|   |  |
|:-:|:-|
| **Alignment** | Evil |
| **Symbol** | Hay |
| **Plane** | The Verdant Cradle |

Artume, the dark and enigmatic goddess, roams the ancient forests as a harbinger of malevolence. Cloaked in shadows, she moves with an ethereal grace, her presence causing the rustle of leaves and the murmurs of the woodland to fall eerily silent. Her eyes, like cold embers, burn with a bitter resolve.

Once bound in lawful union to Liod, the cold and distant Patron of Winter, Artume learned to love with restraint and to endure absence as doctrine. Yet Liod’s gaze strayed to Lirathiel, her own sister, whose courtly tenderness and aching devotion offered what winter never could: warmth that lingered, and a presence that demanded to be felt. From that betrayal, Artume’s heart did not break openly. It hardened.

Bearing an intense disdain for unchecked intrusion and untempered passion, Artume views such forces as abominations born of imbalance, the same imbalance that poisoned her household and scarred the Verdant Cradle. She seeks to proliferate malignant spirits throughout the forests, twisting the natural order into a domain of dark enchantments where only the cunning and restrained may endure. Her influence stirs unrest, as creatures of the night rally to her call, sowing discord and fear among those who trespass, and among those who mistake longing for right.

Those who venture too close to her realm sense the oppressive weight of her wrath, a chilling reminder of ancient grievances she refuses to relinquish. Artume’s relentless campaign of purification is driven by a belief that her domain, like her vows, must be preserved through severity, even if it demands cruelty. This makes her both a formidable adversary and a tragic figure, bound by principles hardened into obsession. Her story serves as a cautionary tale of restraint turned absolute, and of the destructive potential of conviction when it leaves no room for mercy, nor any path back to her sister, nor to the god who made winter of her marriage.

## Auctorion
##### *God of Authority, The Crowned Will, Sovereign of Ambition*

{{imageMaskEdge3,--offset:5%,--rotation:270
![Auctorion](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/la-rockoteque/Vestigium/refs/heads/main/assets/art/Dieties/Auctorion.png){position:absolute,top:0,right:-20%,height:100%}
}}

|   |  |
|:-:|:-|
| **Alignment** | Evil |
| **Symbol** | A vertical sigil of three ascending bars crowned by an unbroken circle |
| **Plane** | The Throne of Ambitions |
| **Slogan** | Authority is not taken. It is acknowledged. |

Auctorion, God of the Imperatum, rules from the Throne of Ambitions, a seat not carved from stone or metal, but from resolve made manifest. His presence bends posture and quiets dissent before a word is spoken. Where he stands, hierarchy asserts itsAestari instinctively, and even rivals find their voices measured, their defiance weighed before utterance. He does not shout commands. He makes obedience feel inevitable.

His ascent was not born solely of will, however absolute that will became. In the age before his coronation, when ambition still lacked form, it was Caprico who guided him through the unseen thresholds of recognition. Caprico did not grant power, nor did he crown Auctorion. Instead, he revealed the hidden mechanisms by which authority is acknowledged rather than seized: the rituals of assent, the symbols that compel belief, the moment when aspiration hardens into mandate.

What Caprico offered was not dominion, but legibility. Through his counsel, Auctorion learned how ambition must be made visible to be obeyed, how hierarchy survives not through force alone, but through the careful shaping of expectation. When Auctorion finally took the Throne of Ambitions, it was understood that he had earned it, yet it was Caprico who ensured the world would recognize that earning as law.

Crowned in sigils of office rather than jewels, Auctorion appears as a figure of immaculate bearing, neither monstrous nor warm, but undeniably sovereign. His gaze carries the weight of judgment rendered without malice or mercy, for authority, in his doctrine, is not emotion, but function. To rule is not to desire control, but to become the axis around which others align.

Caprico does not stand beside him now, nor does Auctorion invoke his name. Their pact was one of passage, not permanence. Yet among those who study power closely, it is said that every throne requires not only ambition to claim it, but a witness who understands how ambition becomes rule. In that truth, Caprico’s influence lingers, even as Auctorion reigns alone.

| **Plane** | The Lithic Bastion |
| **Followers** | Dwarven architects, stone-carving ascetics, gemcutters who hear “the song beneath the stone. |

Azromar is carved from the first stone that ever cooled. He slumbers and wakes with the tectonic rhythm of eternity. Dwelling deep within the Lithic Bastion, a plane of colossal caverns, diamond peaks, and basalt skies, Azromar is unmoving yet ever-present.

His form is vast and multifaceted, a living monolith whose skin resembles polished granite veined with veins of sapphire and ruby. His voice is a tremor; his footsteps are fault lines. He speaks little, but his teachings are etched into the world’s bones.

He is revered by builders, miners, and those who seek unyielding strength in the face of time.

## Bhaz’qorith
##### *the Unraveler*

{{imageMaskEdge3,--offset:5%,--rotation:270
![Bhaz’qorith](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/la-rockoteque/Vestigium/refs/heads/main/assets/art/Dieties/Bhaz%E2%80%99qorith.png){position:absolute,top:0,right:-20%,height:100%}
}}

|   |  |
|:-:|:-|
| **Alignment** | Evil |
| **Symbol** | Gaping Mouth |
| **Pantheon** | Vorthirith |
| **Plane** | The Far Cosmos |

Bhaz’qorith was once Vorthirith, kin to Abhaaldrac, but where Abhaaldrac observes, Bhaz’qorith consumes. Drawn to the birthing Intent of Acrimony, he was twisted by its pulse, reshaped into a force that does not destroy matter, but unwinds meaning.

His form is a wound in reality, a spiraling mouth of teeth and sinew where structure collapses. Wherever Bhaz’qorith passes, pillars crack not from force, but from incompatibility. Threads fray, oaths dissolve, identities lose coherence. He is not entropy as decay, but entropy as unmaking.

It was through Bhaz’qorith that Véa was violated, an act neither romantic nor accidental. From that rupture, life was scattered through the Verdant Cradle, seeded not by design, but by consequence. Bhaz’qorith does not understand creation, yet he is forced to witness it endure, growing defiantly from the scars he leaves behind.

This is his torment. He unravels, yet cannot prevent the emergence of form. Every forest, every cultivated field, every cycle of restraint stands as quiet defiance against him. His hunger remains, but his certainty does not.

## C'thithr'zho
##### *the Lamenter of All Things*

{{imageMaskEdge5,--offset:5%,--rotation:90
![C'thithr'zho](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/la-rockoteque/Vestigium/refs/heads/main/assets/art/Dieties/C%27thithr%27zho.png){position:absolute,top:0,left:-20%,height:100%}
}}

|   |  |
|:-:|:-|
| **Alignment** | Evil |
| **Pantheon** | Vorthirith |
| **Plane** | The Far Cosmos |

Where Abhaaldrac wonders and Bhaz’qorith unravels, C’thithr’zho grieves.

He does not destroy out of curiosity or appetite. He consumes because despair must be completed. Vast beyond comprehension, C’thithr’zho drifts through the Far Cosmos as a living requiem, his presence draining hope before flesh. Songs falter, prayers lose their voice, and even memory begins to erode beneath his shadow.

Planets do not merely die beneath him; they accept their end. Oceans darken, skies collapse inward, and civilizations surrender not in terror, but in silence. To encounter C’thithr’zho is to understand inevitability not as violence, but as sorrow fulfilled.

It is said that Dunamedall was created in response to him, a being capable of holding confinement and consequence where despair would otherwise consume all meaning. If Bhaz’qorith threatens structure and Abhaaldrac threatens purpose, C’thithr’zho threatens endurance itsAestari.

He does not rage. He does not celebrate. He laments, and in that lament lies annihilation.

## Callista

{{imageMaskEdge3,--offset:5%,--rotation:270
![Callista](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/la-rockoteque/Vestigium/refs/heads/main/assets/art/Dieties/Callista.png){position:absolute,top:0,right:-20%,height:100%}
}}

|   |  |
|:-:|:-|
| **Alignment** | Good |
| **Symbol** | The Chalice |
| **Pantheon** | Huruma's Offspring |
| **Plane** | The Throne of Ambitions |

The poor and the forsaken worship Callista, for she is the mistress of giving. She is the patron of those who have nothing yet still offer kindness, the protector of those who endure hardship with grace. Her followers often take vows of poverty, dedicating themsAestaris to service and charity.

Men have long depicted her as a woman baring her breast to feed a child, a symbol of nurture and generosity. Many erotic paintings have been made in her likeness, though such interpretations stray far from her true nature. She does not embrace sexuality or any form of carnal worship, nor does she seek adoration for the sake of desire. To Callista, giving is a sacred act, not an indulgence.

{{imageMaskEdge5,--offset:5%,--rotation:90
![Canuk](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/la-rockoteque/Vestigium/refs/heads/main/assets/art/Dieties/Canuk.png){position:absolute,top:0,left:-20%,height:100%}
}}

|   |  |
|:-:|:-|
| **Alignment** | Good |
| **Symbol** | Book |
| **Plane** | Everbright |

Canuk, known as The Librarian, is a god revered as the guardian of knowledge and the keeper of infinite wisdom. Within his vast, ethereal library, boundless bookshAestaris stretch endlessly, each tome capturing the collective knowledge and secrets of the world. Canuk's domain is a sanctuary of learning, filled with the quiet rustle of turning pages and the gentle glow of reading lamps that flicker with enchanted light.

In myth, Canuk's search for understanding led him to a poignant and unreciprocated romance with Ophelie, a figure of captivating allure. This tale of longing and unfulfilled love inspired a theatrical play, celebrated for its depth but controversially banned across the world due to its sensitive portrayal of divine emotions. Despite its prohibition, the play remains beloved by Ophelie and Roma, celebrated in clandestine circles for its exploration of love and the eternal pursuit of wisdom.

Canuk, with his infinite library, continues to embody the pursuit of knowledge, inspiring scholars and seekers who value learning above all else. His legend serves as a testament to the complexities of desire and the pursuit of enlightenment.

{{imageMaskEdge3,--offset:5%,--rotation:270

|   |  |
|:-:|:-|
| **Alignment** | Good |
| **Symbol** | Billy Goat Head |

Caprico, the enigmatic Guardian of Time, stands as the vigilant protector of the delicate threads that weave the continuity of existence. His presence is both ethereal and commanding, ensuring that time's relentless flow remains unbroken despite the myriad threats that seek to unravel its fabric. With the head of a billy goat, Caprico embodies both the wisdom of age and the vitality of nature, his inscrutable gaze peering through the ages with unwavering focus.

When Caprico appears, time itsAestari seems to pause, drawing attention to the fragility of the moments he safeguards. In his wake, he leaves a trail of black blossoms, strange and beautiful reminders of his passage, each petal a silent testament to the mysteries of time that he guards so fiercely. These flowers, as ephemeral as the seconds they represent, whisper the tales of epochs past and futures yet to be.

His existence is essential, a cornerstone ensuring that the past, present, and future remain harmonious. Those who witness Caprico are left with a profound sense of awe, cognizant of his ceaseless duty and the preciousness of each fleeting moment he protects.

## Cassian
##### *First Prosometron*

{{imageMaskEdge5,--offset:5%,--rotation:90
![Cassian](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/la-rockoteque/Vestigium/refs/heads/main/assets/art/Dieties/Cassian.png){position:absolute,top:0,left:-20%,height:100%}
}}

|   |  |
|:-:|:-|
| **Alignment** | Evil |
| **Symbol** | A triangle, in a square, in a circle |
| **Pantheon** | Prosometron |
| **Plane** | The Infinite Loom |
| **Slogan** | The Prosometron do not end humanity. They ensure it does not arrive too early |

Cassian is remembered as the first Prosometron to be witnessed and recorded. Of all his kind, his visage bears the closest approximation to human proportion, though even this resemblance is faint and unsettling. His geometry is softer, his symmetry less severe, as if his function required initial recognition.

Cassian’s reaping was quiet. Those taken by him fell gently, as though released from exhaustion. In later ages, scholars would debate whether this gentleness reflected intent or merely proximity to the beginning of the measure.

Cassian established the pattern by which the Prosometron are understood: appearance without announcement, action without cruelty, departure without trace. After Cassian, humanity learned that extinction need not arrive through catastrophe to be complete.

## Corvin
##### *Third Prosometron*

{{imageMaskEdge3,--offset:5%,--rotation:270
![Corvin](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/la-rockoteque/Vestigium/refs/heads/main/assets/art/Dieties/Corvin.png){position:absolute,top:0,right:-20%,height:100%}

Those who glimpsed Yagnar in his earliest days spoke of a presence that bent the mind as much as it crushed the body. His visage was said to radiate violence made flesh, a living proof that creation without restraint breeds monstrosity. Fearing what they had wrought, the gods conspired to bind him, casting Yagnar into a hidden prison upon an unnamed and unreachable realm, where his rage could not fracture the ordered worlds.

Yet imprisonment did not diminish him. In isolation, Yagnar’s fury sharpened into doctrine. He became the silent patron of endurance through cruelty, of obedience enforced by fear, and of strength proven only through suffering. To the Vorrakis, sons of the Duns as he is, Yagnar represents not shame but origin: the truth that they were never meant to be gentle, only unbroken. His divinity is not worshipped through prayer alone, but through conquest, scars, and the acceptance of a brutal inheritance the world would rather deny.
